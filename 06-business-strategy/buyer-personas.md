# Buyer Personas — SuccessOS AI

---

## Primary Buyer: CS Director / VP Customer Success

**Representative name:** Raj Mehta, VP Customer Success, Series C SaaS, $40M ARR

**Role:**
- Owns NRR, churn rate, and expansion revenue targets
- Manages 15–50 CSMs
- Reports to CRO or CEO

**Goals:**
- Reduce surprise churn (churn that wasn't forecasted)
- Scale the CS team without linear headcount growth
- Show CS as a revenue-generating function, not a cost center
- Build a data-driven CS organization

**Daily pain:**
- Knows which accounts are probably at risk, but relies on CSMs to tell them — and CSMs are inconsistent
- QBR prep takes each CSM 3–5 hours per quarter; quality varies wildly
- No early warning system — renewal risk is discovered 45–60 days before renewal, not 6 months before
- Cannot see across all accounts at once; spends 4–6 hours/week in 1:1s asking "how is [account] doing?"

**Motivations to buy:**
- Demonstrably reduce churn (they have a churn target on their performance plan)
- Save 30–40% of CSM admin time so team can spend more time in customer conversations
- Be able to report to CEO with confidence: "Here is our at-risk account list and our action plan"

**Objections:**
- "We tried a churn prediction tool before and the data quality was bad" → Response: We don't just score accounts — we show you the signals so you can verify
- "Our CSMs will resist another tool" → Response: We reduce their work, not add to it; QBR prep goes from 4 hours to 30 minutes
- "We're already paying for [CRM]" → Response: Your CRM doesn't read meeting notes and doesn't write QBRs

**Buying process:**
- Signs off on the deal; sometimes recommends upward to CRO
- Needs business case quantified (hours saved + expected churn recovery)
- Typically wants a pilot before full commitment

## Economic Buyer: CRO / COO

**Representative name:** Dev Malhotra, CRO, $80M ARR B2B SaaS

**Role:**
- Owns full revenue number (Sales + CS + Expansion)
- Makes budget decisions for the CS tech stack
- Rarely involved in day-to-day CS operations

**Goals:**
- Maximize net revenue retention
- Ensure CS is a strategic revenue function
- Reduce cost of churn relative to new ARR

**Triggers for involvement:**
- High-value deal (Enterprise tier, > $100K contract)
- Strategic account at risk (churn could materially affect revenue)
- Renewal rate has been a topic at board level

**What they need to see:**
- ROI calculation: expected churn reduction × average contract value
- Time to value: how quickly will we see results?
- Risk: what happens if adoption is poor?

**Messaging:**
"For a $30M ARR company with 12% annual churn, reducing churn by 2 points is $600K in retained ARR. SuccessOS AI typically pays for itself in one saved renewal."

## Champion: CS Operations Manager

**Representative name:** Priya Nair, CS Operations, 30-person CS team

**Role:**
- Manages tooling, reporting, and process for the CS team
- Often the person who evaluates and implements new CS tools
- Reports to VP CS

**Goals:**
- Reduce manual reporting work
- Build a scalable CS tech stack
- Make the CSM team more efficient

**Why they champion SuccessOS AI:**
- They're the one who currently builds the "at-risk account" spreadsheet manually every week
- They know the current process is broken; they want to fix it
- Implementation success makes them a hero

**Role in the deal:**
- Often initiates the evaluation process
- Runs the pilot
- Primary integration point with IT and CRM team
- Internal advocate to VP CS

**What they need:**
- Implementation documentation
- Integration support
- Clear evidence the system works (pilot results)

## End User: Customer Success Manager

**Representative name:** Maya Chen, CSM, managing 35 accounts, $2.8M ARR book

**Role:**
- Day-to-day account management
- Owns QBRs, renewals, and expansion conversations for their book

**Goals:**
- Keep their book healthy
- Not miss a renewal risk
- Spend more time in customer conversations, less time on admin

**Pain:**
- QBR prep is the worst part of the job — takes too long, feels like it doesn't help the customer
- Cannot stay on top of all 35 accounts; some get attention, some get neglected
- No system tells them when an account is getting quiet

**What makes them love the product:**
- Meeting analysis saves them the "what do I write?" moment after every call
- QBR generator means QBR prep is reviewing, not writing
- Action recommendations tell them what to do next without having to figure it out

**Adoption risk:**
- CSMs who feel the AI will "replace them" will resist
- Mitigated by: positioning AI as their assistant, not their replacement; all customer actions still require their approval and judgment

## Anti-Personas (Who We're Not Selling To)

| Profile | Why Not ICP |
|---------|------------|
| CS Director at a services company | Not a SaaS recurring revenue model |
| CS Director with <5 CSMs | Too small for AI value; volume not sufficient |
| VP CS at a company with no CRM | Integration surface doesn't exist |
| CS Director in the seat < 3 months | No budget authority yet; wait for next cycle |
